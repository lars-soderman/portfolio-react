Introduction

Bikelab

Bikelab is a concept for true mountainbiking enthusiasts who want to fully customize their bike builds, right down to the nittiest gritty.

This is a project i did as part of the 12 week upgrade course Digital design at Berghs School of communication. The research phase was done in pairs, the rest individually.

Research

We were given the task of improving an existing bicycle online store. We started out by interviewing random bicyclists on the street and found that in general the willingness to purchase bicyles online seemed quite low, and from talking to trade representatives we learned that online sales only make up around 5–10% of the total market. As we visited the physical store (↑) and interviewed its owner we learned that the niche of high end mountainbikes and racers had its own set of mechanisms, with prices 10-20x higher than the average new bike sold in Sweden.

When I was 13, I won a mountainbiking competition - the first one I ever entered! I just had to humble brag about this as i asked for help with my survey in the Facebook-group “Vi som älskar mountainbike”

We started looking more closely at mountainbiking enthusiasts, trying to create insights into their behaviour and motivations. A targeted online survey revealed that their level of consumption was pretty extreme, with an average price for a new bike of around 30.000 SEK (approx 5x higher than the average bike), a yearly spend of 6.000 SEK on accessories and parts, and and average of 5 new bikes purchased in the last 10 years. over 80% of participants stated they would be willing to purchase a new bike online.

Furthermore, a recurring theme in the survey answers was the high level of knowledge and motivation to do their own research about all the different bikes, brands and parts available. And a common reason for not wanting to visit physical stores was (paraphrased) “the clerks have less knowledge than I, and besides that they have a shitty attitude”

The survey actually made it all the way to “Sportfack”, a magazine for Swedish sports retailers. Look mom!

Analysis

We formed a hypothesis that based on the insights above, enthusiasts would be positive to a service that would let them put toghether their own bikes and be able to customize every detail. The bigger the variety of brands, the better. Technical info such as weight and price would be displayed and instantly updated as options are changed. The bike would be delivered either in parts, or optionally they would be assembled at a local service partner. This hypothesis was validated using input from the same community that

Up until about this point, i also entertained an alternative idea for a super cool concept which was a sort of subscription for kids bikes. So as the kids grow, you´d be able to have the out-grown bike picked up and replaced with a larger size. Wouldnt that be awesome? Anyway, that idea didnt make the cut so I shouldnt keep going on about it.

Delivery

Based on the motivations and attitudes surfaced in the survey and interviews, i created a moodboard that aimed to capture the important values: logic, playfulness, do-it-yourself, attention to detail, and a sense of rebellion.

I created "high-ish-res" wireframes and continued with user testing to try to find out how all the steps should fit together, which actions needed an "are you sure"-question (I'm sure there is a name for that by the way!) and so on.

The test subjects also gave me some great ideas that I didn't have time to pursue, for example the possibility to add products to a kind of a short-list of options for each slot.

For this project I focused on the specific interaction of changing the fork on one of your active Bikelab-projects. This is a screen shot of the Sketch-file.

I also made a clickable high-res prototype in Protopie (love that app!), I hope I get around to adding a video of that here soon.

Conclusion

In conclusion this was actually a super awesome project. And such a total luxury to be able to spend 4 weeks in research before deciding on the problem definition. And also being allowed to set aside all the logistical issues that I suppose would be involved in actually launching a service like this. But, you know, all those limitations are based in how bike manufacturers and bike retailers do business right now, and I think that if this indeed is the way mountainbike enthusiasts want to buy their bikes, then it is only a matter of time. Guess we'll see!
